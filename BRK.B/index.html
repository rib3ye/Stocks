BRK.B Berkshire Hathaway Inc.

Stock research reference · Research date: April 6, 2026

Company Overview

Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (NYSE: BRK.B) is an American multinational conglomerate holding company that owns and operates over 60 major subsidiaries spanning insurance (GEICO, General Re, Berkshire Hathaway Reinsurance Group), freight rail transportation (BNSF Railway), energy and utilities (Berkshire Hathaway Energy), manufacturing (Precision Castparts, Lubrizol, Marmon Group, IMC/Iscar), retail and consumer brands (See's Candies, Dairy Queen, Duracell, Fruit of the Loom), and wholesale distribution (McLane Company). The company also maintains one of the world's largest equity investment portfolios, with major stakes in Apple, Bank of America, American Express, Coca-Cola, Chevron, and Occidental Petroleum. Berkshire serves markets across virtually every sector of the economy, with approximately 86% of revenue derived from U.S. operations and the remainder from Europe, Asia-Pacific, and Canada. The company's defining competitive characteristics are its decentralized operating model — a corporate headquarters of only ~27 employees overseeing 387,800 total employees — and its massive insurance float, which provides low-cost capital for investments and acquisitions.

Berkshire Hathaway traces its origins to 1839, when Oliver Chace founded a textile operation in Valley Falls, Rhode Island. The modern entity was formed in 1955 through the merger of Berkshire Fine Spinning Associates and Hathaway Manufacturing Company. Warren Buffett began purchasing shares at $7.60 per share in 1962 through Buffett Partnership Limited, eventually gaining control of the company on May 10, 1965. Berkshire was not a traditional IPO — it was already publicly traded on the OTC market as a textile manufacturer. Class A shares (BRK.A) were listed on the NYSE on November 29, 1988. Class B shares (BRK.B) were created on May 9, 1996, at 1/30th the Class A value (subsequently split 50-for-1 in 2010). Warren Buffett remains the company's largest individual shareholder, holding approximately 227,000 Class A shares (~37% of Class A outstanding), representing roughly 30% of total voting power and a ~15% economic interest, valued at approximately $149 billion as of late 2025.

Business Model

Berkshire Hathaway's business model is built on a unique capital allocation flywheel: insurance subsidiaries collect premiums and generate "float" (money held between premium collection and claim payment), which provides essentially free capital to deploy into acquisitions and equity investments. This structure gives Berkshire a significant cost-of-capital advantage over peers. The company operates with extreme decentralization — subsidiary CEOs run their businesses with near-total autonomy, submitting only monthly financial statements and remitting excess cash to Omaha. Corporate headquarters has only ~27 employees, compared to thousands at peer conglomerates. Berkshire's SG&A expenses were approximately $43.5B in 2025 (~11.7% of revenue), though this is dominated by subsidiary-level costs; the corporate overhead is negligible.

Revenue composition is overwhelmingly from operating businesses rather than investment income. Insurance premiums contribute ~28% of total revenue, manufacturing ~21%, service and retailing ~12%, railroad ~6%, and energy/utilities ~7%. Geographically, approximately 86% of revenue is domestic and 14% international (Europe, Asia-Pacific, Canada, Latin America). Key distribution relationships include McLane's role as a major supplier to Walmart, GEICO's direct-to-consumer model, BNSF's connections to all major North American ports, and BHE's regulated utility franchise territories.

CEO Profile

Background

Gregory Edward Abel (b. June 1, 1962, Edmonton, Alberta, Canada) earned a Bachelor of Commerce degree in accounting from the University of Alberta in 1984 and is an AICPA-certified public accountant. He began his career at PricewaterhouseCoopers in Edmonton, later transferring to the firm's San Francisco office, where he was recruited by CalEnergy (a geothermal power company and PwC client) to run its accounting operations. In 1996, CalEnergy purchased an electric utility in England, and Abel relocated to manage the operation. When the company acquired MidAmerican Energy in Des Moines, Iowa in 1999 and adopted its name, Abel returned to the U.S. as president. He was named CEO of MidAmerican Energy (later Berkshire Hathaway Energy) in 2008.

Abel was named Warren Buffett's designated successor during Berkshire's May 2025 annual meeting and officially became CEO on January 1, 2026. His Berkshire holdings include ~249 Class A shares and ~2,363 Class B shares, valued at approximately $175 million [verify], representing well under 0.01% of shares outstanding. His net worth is estimated at approximately $1 billion (Bloomberg Billionaires Index, January 2026), largely derived from the 2022 sale of his ~1% stake in Berkshire Hathaway Energy back to Berkshire for approximately $870 million. Abel has pledged to invest his entire after-tax CEO salary (~$15M/year) in Berkshire stock. He is an avid hockey enthusiast — his uncle Sid Abel was a Hockey Hall of Famer — and received the Horatio Alger Award in 2018.

Investment Implications

  • Shareholder alignment improving but still modest: Abel's ~$175M stake is negligible relative to BRK's $1T+ market cap, but his pledge to invest his full after-tax salary signals growing alignment over time.
  • Key-person risk shifted from extreme to elevated: The Buffett-to-Abel transition addresses the most acute succession risk, but Abel is untested in capital allocation at scale. Buffett remains available as advisor.
  • Governance stronger than it appears: Decentralized model and strong board provide structural resilience, but minimal investor communication cadence (no calls, no guidance) remains a relative black box vs. peers.
  • PacifiCorp wildfire exposure is tangible: ~$48B in pending claims is material even for Berkshire. How Abel resolves this will define his early reputation.
  • Strategic continuity is the base case: Abel emphasizes stewardship and discipline. The risk is stasis — whether he can adapt Berkshire's approach without Buffett's intuitive pattern-matching.

Competitive Position & Moat

  • Insurance float as a zero-cost permanent capital engine: Berkshire's insurance operations generated approximately $176 billion in float as of year-end 2025. Float functions as a massive pool of investable capital that Berkshire deploys into equities, bonds, and wholly-owned businesses. Critically, Berkshire's disciplined underwriting has historically produced this float at a negative cost: GEICO posted a combined ratio of 84.7% in 2025 and the overall insurance group operated profitably. By contrast, Markel (MKL) maintains ~$19B in float with a 93–95% combined ratio, and Fairfax (FFH) holds $39.3B in float at a 93.0% combined ratio. The sheer magnitude of $176B in free capital took six decades to build and is essentially impossible for any competitor to replicate.
  • Permanent capital structure with no redemption risk: Unlike PE funds, hedge funds, or banks, Berkshire faces no liquidity calls, fund redemptions, or depositor runs. Its equity capital comes from retained earnings and insurance float, not from limited partners who can demand money back in a downturn. JPMorgan Chase (JPM), despite its $4T balance sheet, operates under Basel III constraints and stress-test requirements that limit how aggressively it can deploy capital into illiquid, long-duration assets. Berkshire ended 2025 holding approximately $373B in cash — extraordinary optionality to act as a buyer of last resort during financial dislocations, as it did in 2008–2009 with Goldman Sachs and Bank of America.
  • Decentralized conglomerate model with radical operational autonomy: Berkshire runs ~27 employees at corporate headquarters overseeing a conglomerate of ~400,000 people across 60+ subsidiaries. Subsidiary CEOs operate with near-total autonomy: no strategic plans to submit, no corporate meetings to attend. They send monthly financials and excess cash to Omaha. This produces extraordinarily low corporate overhead. Markel (MKL) has adopted a similar philosophy but operates at roughly one-fiftieth of Berkshire's scale. No traditional conglomerate (GE, Honeywell, Danaher) operates with this level of decentralization.
  • Unmatched scale and flexibility in capital allocation: Berkshire's combination of $373B cash, $176B float, and hundreds of billions in equity holdings gives it a capital deployment capacity no other single entity can match. It can write a $10B reinsurance check, acquire a $30B railroad, or invest $5B in a distressed institution — sometimes within days. Private equity firms can raise comparable sums but must return capital within fund lifetimes and depend on leverage. Fairfax (FFH) and Markel (MKL) cannot credibly compete for Berkshire-scale acquisitions.
  • Tax efficiency through deferred capital gains: Berkshire's buy-and-hold philosophy generates a massive deferred tax liability — estimated at over $60B [verify] — that functions as an interest-free loan. By holding appreciated positions for decades rather than trading, Berkshire avoids realizing taxable gains and allows pre-tax capital to compound at a higher base. Mutual funds and hedge funds face annual distribution requirements that force tax realization.
  • Brand and reputation in deal sourcing: The Berkshire name — built over Buffett's 60-year tenure — carries a reputational premium in deal origination. Family-owned businesses actively seek Berkshire as an acquirer for its reputation of preserving independence, retaining management, and never reselling. Some of Berkshire's best acquisitions (BNSF, Precision Castparts, Lubrizol) came through direct approaches where cultural fit gave it an edge over PE bidders offering higher prices. The key risk is whether this halo survives the Buffett-to-Abel transition.

Synthesis

Berkshire Hathaway possesses what is arguably the widest economic moat of any publicly traded company — not because of any single advantage, but because of a self-reinforcing system of structural advantages that compounds over time and resists imitation. The moat is primarily economic and behavioral in nature: zero-cost insurance float provides permanent, non-callable capital; a decentralized model with negligible corporate overhead keeps returns high; a buy-and-hold tax deferral strategy compounds pre-tax dollars at a higher base; and a decades-old reputation attracts proprietary deal flow. Each element reinforces the others. The primary threats are not competitive (no rival can replicate 60 years of compounding) but organizational: the sheer scale of $373B in cash creates a mathematical headwind to future returns, and regulatory or tax-policy changes could structurally narrow the advantages Berkshire has exploited.

The central strategic question for Berkshire's next decade is whether the system Warren Buffett built can perpetuate itself without Warren Buffett. Greg Abel is respected for his operational discipline at BHE and has pledged to maintain the decentralized model and patient capital allocation philosophy. Early signals are cautiously positive: Abel has retained existing management, Ajit Jain continues overseeing insurance, and Buffett remains as chairman. However, Abel must prove he can deploy capital at attractive returns when the next major opportunity arises — leaving $373B idle in T-bills indefinitely will erode per-share compounding. And the culture of trust binding 60+ subsidiaries must hold without Buffett's gravitational pull. On the competitive side, Progressive's (PGR) superior telematics-driven underwriting and faster premium growth represent a genuine threat to GEICO, and Fairfax (FFH) under Prem Watsa has demonstrated the float-funded conglomerate model can be executed at smaller scale with potentially higher growth rates. Berkshire's moat remains exceptionally wide, but its future width depends less on the architecture — which is sound — and more on whether the new steward can operate the machinery with the same discipline that built it.

Regulatory & Geopolitical Context

Regulatory Landscape

Berkshire operates under an unusually broad regulatory umbrella. Its insurance operations are regulated primarily at the state level under the NAIC framework. The NAIC's 2026 priorities include heightened scrutiny of insurer investment strategies (particularly private credit), updated risk-based capital requirements, and a new Invested Assets Task Force replacing the Valuation of Securities Task Force as of January 2026. On the federal level, the CFPB sued Berkshire's Vanderbilt Mortgage subsidiary in January 2025 for predatory lending in manufactured housing, but the suit was voluntarily dismissed in February 2025 as part of the administration's CFPB retrenchment. Berkshire has historically avoided SIFI designation, and the current deregulatory posture makes such designation even less likely.

BNSF Railway is subject to Surface Transportation Board oversight, though the STB's withdrawal of the FORR rule (struck down on appeal) reduces regulatory pressure on railroad pricing. BNSF is investing $3.6B in its network for 2026. Berkshire Hathaway Energy is regulated by FERC and state PUCs; FERC approved BHE's Midcontinent Grid Solutions transmission joint venture formula rate in September 2025. BHE's $30.8B capex plan for 2024–2026 focuses on renewables, storage, and transmission. Revised HSR merger notification rules (effective Feb 2025) impose more burdensome disclosure for large acquisitions, which could modestly slow Berkshire's deal pace.

On balance, the regulatory environment is moderately favorable. The deregulatory tilt — CFPB retrenchment, reduced SIFI risk, accommodating antitrust stance — benefits a large diversified conglomerate. NAIC-level insurance regulation is tightening around investment risk, but Berkshire's conservative balance sheet positions it as a compliance leader. The principal regulatory risk lies in BHE's energy operations, where state PUC rate cases and FERC transmission cost disputes could constrain returns.

Geopolitical Factors

The dominant geopolitical factor is the tariff regime. The Supreme Court's February 2026 ruling in Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump struck down IEEPA-based tariffs in a 6–3 decision. The administration pivoted to Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, imposing a maximum 15% universal tariff (limited to 150 days). Approximately $166B in previously collected tariffs must be refunded. The average import duty remains ~10% — roughly four times pre-2025 levels. Berkshire's diverse manufacturing and retail subsidiaries face input cost pressures; Berkshire warned in Q2 2025 of "adverse consequences on most, if not all, of our operating businesses." Warren Buffett publicly characterized tariffs as an economic "act of war."

Berkshire's most significant international exposure is its $43.8B position in five Japanese trading houses (Itochu, Marubeni, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo) on a cost basis of $15.4B, generating ~$862M in annual dividends. This position is currency-hedged through ~¥2 trillion in yen-denominated bonds. Abel continued building the position, adding $1.8B in Tokio Marine in early 2026. The moderately elevated rate environment benefits Berkshire's $176B float invested primarily in short-duration treasuries, though insurance investment income declined to $12.5B in 2025 from $13.7B in 2024.

Industry Tailwinds & Headwinds

Several macro trends favor Berkshire. The insurance industry is coming off a strong hard market cycle, with GEICO holding 11.56% of U.S. auto insurance market share and reporting $2.2B in Q1 2025 pre-tax underwriting earnings. The elevated rate environment generates meaningful investment income on float. U.S. infrastructure spending directly benefits BHE and BNSF. Berkshire's $373B cash hoard provides unmatched optionality for distressed situations.

The headwinds are real but manageable. The seven-year commercial hard market is softening — property rates expected to fall 0–5% for clean risks in H1 2026, E&S catastrophe-exposed rates declining ~10%. Abel has guided that Berkshire will write less P&C reinsurance premium "so long as these phases of the cycle endure." Climate-driven catastrophe losses remain structural: 2025 saw $129B in global insured losses, with the Los Angeles wildfires alone generating $41B. Tariff-induced cost inflation impacts manufacturing subsidiaries, with the average U.S. household facing an estimated $1,500 tariff tax increase in 2026.

Net Assessment

The environment for Berkshire is mixed but net positive. The deregulatory trend reduces compliance burden; the Supreme Court's tariff ruling materially reduced trade uncertainty; elevated rates support float income; and $373B in cash provides unmatched flexibility. Key uncertainties: (1) pace of insurance soft market compression, (2) economic impact of residual tariffs on manufacturing/retail, (3) climate-driven catastrophe losses trending structurally higher, and (4) Abel's ability to deploy cash at returns justifying the opportunity cost. This assessment would turn negative if a severe recession materialized, catastrophe losses spiked sharply, or Congress enacted legislation targeting large conglomerates or re-establishing SIFI oversight for non-bank financial institutions.

Risks

  • Post-Buffett execution risk: Greg Abel became CEO on January 1, 2026, inheriting an organization whose competitive advantages were built over 60 years by one of history's most successful investors. Abel's background is in energy operations, not capital allocation at scale. The critical question is whether he can deploy $373B in cash at returns that exceed the opportunity cost of Treasury bills. His first major move (OxyChem at $9.7B) was pre-negotiated during Buffett's tenure. The first truly Abel-originated large acquisition will be the defining test.
  • PacifiCorp wildfire liabilities ($48B in claims): PacifiCorp, a Berkshire Hathaway Energy subsidiary, faces approximately $48B in pending claims from the 2020 Oregon wildfires. Projected pretax losses already stand at ~$2.75B, with S&P warning of potential junk-status downgrade. Abel's legislative strategy to cap utility liability is politically contentious and may not succeed across all jurisdictions. This is material even for a company with $373B in cash.
  • Insurance soft market compression: After seven years of hard market pricing, commercial lines are definitively softening — the first decline since 2018. Property rates expected to fall 0–5% in H1 2026, E&S catastrophe-exposed rates declining ~10%. Abel has already guided lower P&C reinsurance premiums. Underwriting profits dropped 54% in Q4 2025. If the soft cycle deepens, insurance earnings — Berkshire's economic engine — face multi-year headwinds.
  • Cash deployment drag on returns: Berkshire's record $373B cash position (~4.9% of the entire U.S. T-bill market) earns conservative short-term yields while equity markets compound at higher rates. Every year this cash sits idle, it represents an opportunity cost that dilutes per-share intrinsic value growth. Berkshire's P/FCF of 41x already reflects the market's uncertainty about whether this cash will be deployed productively.
  • Climate-driven catastrophe losses: Global insured catastrophe losses hit $129B in 2025, the fourth year in five exceeding $100B. The Los Angeles wildfires alone generated $41B in insured losses. Berkshire is one of the world's largest reinsurers by capacity. A single severe catastrophe year (hurricane, earthquake, wildfire) could generate tens of billions in underwriting losses.
  • GEICO competitive pressure from Progressive: Progressive (PGR) has been gaining auto insurance market share through superior telematics-driven underwriting and digital-first distribution, posting a 87.4% combined ratio with 16.3% revenue growth. GEICO's lackluster premium growth (despite excellent profitability) suggests it is ceding market position. The auto insurance market is structurally shifting toward data-driven, usage-based pricing where Progressive leads.
  • Tariff impact on manufacturing and retail subsidiaries: Even after the Supreme Court's IEEPA ruling, residual tariffs averaging ~10% on imports create cost pressures across Berkshire's diverse manufacturing (Precision Castparts, Lubrizol), retail (Dairy Queen, See's), and wholesale (McLane) operations. Berkshire itself warned of "adverse consequences on most, if not all, operating businesses."
  • Concentration of voting power and succession beyond Abel: Warren Buffett retains ~30% of voting power and effective veto authority via Class A shares. His charitable bequest plan will gradually disperse these shares after his passing, creating a potential governance vacuum. Beyond Abel, succession planning for the next generation of leadership is less clear, and the cultural coherence binding 60+ autonomous subsidiaries may weaken over time.

Bullish Case

  • $373B cash war chest provides unmatched strategic optionality: Berkshire enters the post-Buffett era with the largest corporate cash pile in history. In a recession, market dislocation, or financial crisis, Abel can deploy this capital into deeply discounted acquisitions or distressed financing — precisely the playbook that produced some of Berkshire's best historical returns (Goldman Sachs 2008, Bank of America 2011). No other entity on Earth has this combination of capital, speed, and permanent holding period.
  • $176B insurance float at negative cost: Berkshire's insurance float grew to a record $176B in 2025, produced at an underwriting profit (87.1% combined ratio). This is effectively free leverage — policyholders pay Berkshire to hold their money. Combined with elevated interest rates generating investment income on this float, the insurance engine alone justifies a premium valuation.
  • Diversified conglomerate provides earnings resilience: With major operations in insurance, railroads, energy, manufacturing, retail, and a $268B equity portfolio, Berkshire is essentially a diversified economy unto itself. No single sector downturn can break the company. This defensive quality (beta 0.69) is particularly valuable in the current uncertain macro environment.
  • Orderly leadership transition with cultural continuity: The CEO transition from Buffett to Abel was the most anticipated succession in corporate history, and it occurred without disruption. Buffett remains as chairman and daily advisor. Ajit Jain continues overseeing insurance. The decentralized model ensures subsidiaries operate independently of any single leader. Abel's early actions (resuming buybacks, pledging to invest his salary in BRK stock, continuing Japan expansion) signal disciplined stewardship.
  • Japan trading house investments as a compounding engine: The $43.8B position in five Japanese trading houses (on a $15.4B cost basis) represents a 184% unrealized gain while generating ~$862M in annual dividends, currency-hedged via yen-denominated bonds. Abel has signaled a "50 years or forever" holding period. These are high-quality, diversified businesses with strong cash flows and governance improvements.
  • Buyback resumption signals confidence: After a two-year pause, Abel resumed share repurchases in March 2026. With $373B in cash and a policy allowing buybacks when shares trade below intrinsic value, Berkshire has the capacity for massive repurchases if the stock remains below Abel's assessment of fair value. Historically, Buffett's buyback timing has been excellent.
  • Zero SBC and conservative financial culture: In an era of tech companies diluting shareholders by 2–5% annually through stock-based compensation, Berkshire issues virtually zero SBC. This means 100% of earnings accrue to existing shareholders. Combined with no dividend (maximizing tax-efficient compounding) and opportunistic buybacks, Berkshire's capital return framework is exceptionally shareholder-friendly.
  • Trailing P/E of 15.4x for a $1T fortress balance sheet: Berkshire trades at a modest trailing P/E of 15.4x despite holding $373B in cash (36% of market cap), a $268B equity portfolio, and operating earnings of $44.5B. Adjusting for the massive cash position, the operating businesses trade at a significantly lower implied multiple.

Bearish Case

  • Revenue growth has stalled: FY2025 revenue of $371.4B was essentially flat YoY (+0.003%), and operating earnings declined 6.2% to $44.5B. For a $1T company, organic growth levers are limited. Berkshire's diverse subsidiaries include mature, low-growth businesses (railroads, energy utilities, legacy manufacturing) that cannot deliver the double-digit organic growth investors typically associate with mega-cap valuations.
  • Forward P/E of ~23x suggests overvaluation: While the trailing P/E of 15.4x appears modest, the forward P/E of 22.8x [verify] reflects analyst expectations of lower GAAP earnings. This premium may be difficult to sustain in a post-Buffett era where the "Buffett premium" gradually fades and investors demand more transparency and communication than Berkshire's minimal IR approach provides.
  • $373B in T-bills represents massive opportunity cost: The record cash position earns short-term yields (~4–5%) while the S&P 500 has compounded at ~10% annually. Berkshire's P/FCF of 41x reflects the market's skepticism about productive deployment. If Abel cannot find large-scale acquisitions at attractive prices, the cash drag will persistently underperform a simple index fund.
  • Abel is untested in large-scale capital allocation: Greg Abel's career was built in energy operations — regulated utilities, power plants, pipeline negotiations. He has not demonstrated the investor's eye for assessing intrinsic value across industries that defined Buffett's genius. The capital allocation function is Berkshire's core differentiator, and it is the dimension where Abel is most untested.
  • Insurance underwriting profits deteriorating: Q4 2025 insurance underwriting profits dropped 54% YoY, and Abel has explicitly guided for lower P&C reinsurance premiums as the soft cycle takes hold. Insurance investment income also declined from $13.7B to $12.5B. If both underwriting and investment income compress simultaneously, the insurance engine — Berkshire's foundation — could face its weakest period in a decade.
  • GEICO market share erosion: Despite strong profitability, GEICO is losing market share to Progressive, which is growing premiums at 16% while investing in telematics and digital distribution. GEICO's direct model, once revolutionary, is now table stakes. The shift to usage-based insurance pricing favors Progressive's data-driven approach.
  • Mathematical headwind of scale: At $1T market cap, Berkshire needs to generate $100B+ in value to deliver 10% returns. The universe of sufficiently large acquisition targets shrinks with every passing year. Buffett acknowledged this problem repeatedly: "the elephant gun has been reloaded, and my trigger finger is itchy" became a running joke precisely because finding elephant-sized targets grew increasingly difficult.
  • Buffett's charitable bequest creates long-term governance uncertainty: Buffett plans to bequeath 99.5% of his estate (currently ~$149B in BRK stock) to a charitable trust overseen by his three children, which must distribute within 10 years of his passing. This will create sustained selling pressure on BRK shares and eliminate the concentrated voting block that has provided governance stability for decades.

Summary

Berkshire Hathaway enters 2026 as the most uniquely positioned conglomerate in the world: a $1 trillion enterprise with $373B in cash, $176B in zero-cost insurance float, 60+ autonomous subsidiaries spanning nearly every sector, and a decentralized culture built over six decades by Warren Buffett. The bull case centers on this extraordinary optionality — the cash war chest provides unmatched firepower for the next dislocation, the insurance engine produces free capital, the diversified earnings base provides downside resilience, and the trailing P/E of 15.4x undervalues the operating businesses when adjusted for the massive cash position. The bear case is equally substantive: revenue growth has stalled, the forward P/E suggests optimism that may not survive the transition era, Abel is untested in large-scale capital allocation, the insurance soft cycle is compressing the core profit engine, and the mathematical headwind of deploying capital at scale grows steeper every year. The central tension investors must resolve is whether Berkshire's structural advantages — which are genuine and durable — can continue to compound intrinsic value at attractive rates under new leadership, or whether the post-Buffett era marks the beginning of a gradual convergence toward the mean.
